We want to add another game to our cardputer adv firmware, respecting the constraints of the existing firmware design, the power of the hardware, the remaining resources available, and the CYD text-only display connected, similarly to how the "cyberhack" game was implemented.  This game will be a mini clone of Dwarf Fortress, in a Neuromancer‑style cyberpunk city setting, that fits these constraints, including constraint on display and input.  
Game should generate an appropriately small 2D world and jump right into embark mode automatically.  
Unless the user chooses a menu option to reset, every time the game loads it should jump back into the same world where it was left off (autosave to sd card).
Instead of 7 dwarfs to start, just start with 1 character with a randomly generated theme-appropriate name.  The focus will be just on this individual instead of a colony of dwarfs. 
There should be NPC's generated within the world as well, and they will operate with their own algorithmic intelligence that we create and apply to them, following their own motivations, and working within the systems and constructs of this game world.
Rather than building a fortress, the goal for the character here is to start his or her own company within this unforgiving-future cyber world and grow it to an unstoppable megacorp, amassing billions of shards (money).  
He starts as an adult with basically nothing and has to find hourly wage work to survive and accumulate wealth, knowledge, equipment and relationships.  He must find a novice-tier job to assume, learning it from on-the-job experience.
Use a dwarf fortress classic style ascii graphic on the CYD display, with the dwarf fortress style menu system with modal overlays during menu navigation.  
The whole UI should be menu driven with no arrow-cursor selecting things on the actual world map.
To know what points of interest exist on the map rather than arrow over to them, a menu should provide the list, so this is essentially a text-based game but grounded in a world gen.
One of the defining x-factors of dwarf fortress is the way it implements a large number of micro systems that have interaction points between each other to create chain reactions that become story arcs.  We have to build this in, within our limited resource budget.
Example: Hunger → seek food → and then → and then
The thing DF does relentlessly—is that triggers don’t fire in a vacuum.
They fire because micro‑systems constantly create pressure, scarcity, conflict, risk, and opportunity.  On a low‑RAM platform like ESP32, you can’t simulate DF’s giant web of systems, but you can replicate the pattern: tiny, cheap micro‑systems that continuously generate situations that collide with rule‑based actors.
**KEY POINT: None of this game will take place in cyberspace.  Once an entity has a cyberdeck or "deck" they can jack into cyberspace but in that case they should actually play our cyberhack game headless, with automated decision making based on their personality and situation (ex. desperate for shards, nothing to lose- take more risk, or doing well and comfortable- take no risk) and the results of the cyberhack game- shards, relationships, flatline- they feed back into this new game.


THE FOLLOWING IS SUGGESTED REFERENCE CONTENT FOR THE GAME:
Convert *Dwarf Fortress*’s fantasy building blocks into a Neuromancer‑style cyberpunk setting by **mapping each DF system to a cyberpunk analogue**—swap dwarves for street crews, caverns for undercity tunnels, nobles for megacorp fixers, artifacts for black‑market tech, etc.—while keeping the *simulation logic* intact. The trick is to reinterpret DF’s verbs (dig, build, craft, fight, govern) through the lens of Chiba City, Night City, and Sprawl‑era cyberpunk.

Below is a **structured, deep conversion guide** that walks through every major DF subsystem and shows how to re-theme it without breaking the gameplay loop.

---

## Core Takeaway  
**Keep the simulation; change the fiction.**  
Every DF system has a natural cyberpunk equivalent—industrial, gritty, neon‑soaked, corporate‑dominated, and human‑scale. You’re not adding cyberspace gameplay; you’re just acknowledging its existence as a narrative element.

---

## 🧬 Species & Civs → Street Crews, Gangs, Syndicates  




DF races become **factions**, each with culture, gear, and economic niches.

- **Street crews** — your “dwarves.” A tight-knit, resourceful group living in a block, warehouse, or undercity sector.  
- **Megacorp enclaves** — wealthy, militarized, vertically integrated societies.  
- **Yakuza/Triad syndicates** — ritualistic, hierarchical, and territorial.  
- **Nomad caravans** — mobile, scavenger-tech specialists.  
- **Undercity mutants** — DF’s “goblins,” shaped by pollution, biotech, or corporate dumping.

**Gameplay mapping:**  
DF civ → cyberpunk faction with distinct tech, implants, fashion, slang, and economic role.

---

## 🏗️ Fortress Mode → Block/Arcology Management  




Your “fortress” becomes:

- A **derelict arcology floor**  
- A **warehouse complex**  
- A **subway‑adjacent undercity sector**  
- A **sprawl block** with multiple levels (street, mezzanine, roof, tunnels)

**Digging** → cutting through drywall, concrete, ventilation shafts, sewer tunnels.  
**Workshops** → fab-labs, chop shops, biotech clinics, drone bays.  
**Stockpiles** → crates, lockers, cold storage, server racks.

---

## ⚙️ Jobs & Labor → Cyberpunk Trades  
DF’s job system maps beautifully to cyberpunk professions.

- **Ripperdocs** — surgeons for implants (DF medical + crafting).  
- **Techies** — repair, fabrication, electronics.  
- **Scavs** — gather scrap, salvage tech.  
- **Street samurai** — military roles.  
- **Fixers** — nobles/administrators.  
- **Deckers** — DF scholars/priests but with hacking rituals.

**Artifacts** → legendary cyberdecks, prototype implants, experimental weapons.

---

## 🧱 Materials → Industrial & Tech Components  
DF’s stone/metal/wood → cyberpunk equivalents:

- **Concrete, rebar, steel plating**  
- **Circuit boards, fiber optics, microchips**  
- **Bioplastic, carbon composites**  
- **Toxic sludge, industrial waste** (for “evil biome” equivalents)

DF’s metalworking → machining, welding, 3D printing, chip fabrication.

---

## 🏞️ Biomes → City Districts & Undercity Zones  




DF biome → cyberpunk district:

- **Forest → Rooftop gardens, vertical farms**  
- **Desert → Industrial wastelands, megacorp solar fields**  
- **Tundra → Cryo-storage districts, refrigerated logistics hubs**  
- **Caverns → sewer networks, abandoned metro tunnels, utility corridors**  
- **Evil biomes → toxic zones, chemical spills, radiation pockets**

---

## 🐉 Creatures → Gangers, Drones, Mutants  
DF creatures convert cleanly:

- **Wildlife** → stray drones, feral dogs, scavenger bots.  
- **Monsters** → biotech abominations, rogue AIs in robot bodies, vat-grown horrors.  
- **Megabeasts** → colossal industrial machines, runaway construction mechs, giant sewer organisms.

---

## ⚔️ Combat → Street Fights & Corporate Raids  
DF combat simulation stays intact:

- Melee → knives, pipes, machetes, monoblades.  
- Ranged → pistols, SMGs, jury-rigged railguns.  
- Armor → kevlar, ceramic plates, improvised plating.  
- Siege → corp SWAT teams, drone swarms, armored vehicles.

---

## 🧠 Mood System → Cyberpsychosis, Burnout, Addiction  
DF’s emotional simulation maps perfectly:

- **Cyberpsychosis** — too many implants, trauma, overstimulation.  
- **Burnout** — overwork, stress, lack of stim patches.  
- **Addiction** — stims, painkillers, neural enhancers.  
- **Social tension** — gang rivalries, corp pressure, street politics.

Strange moods → manic hacking sessions, obsessive tech builds, implant overclocking.

---

## 🏛️ Nobles → Fixers, Corp Liaisons, Block Bosses  
DF nobles → cyberpunk power brokers:

- **Fixer** — assigns missions, demands tribute.  
- **Corp liaison** — enforces megacorp interests.  
- **Block boss** — local authority figure.  
- **Data priest** — cult-like technomystic leader.

Mandates → “produce 10 cyberdecks,” “no unauthorized implants,” “ban certain drugs.”

---

## 📦 Economy → Black Markets & Supply Chains  
DF trade → cyberpunk logistics:

- **Caravans** → nomad convoys, corp delivery drones, smuggler vans.  
- **Currency** → credsticks, crypto tokens, corp scrip.  
- **Trade goods** → implants, weapons, rare chips, illicit biotech.

---

## 🧪 Magic → Biotech, Implants, Ritualistic Hacking  
DF magic → cyberpunk “soft magic” systems:

- **Implants** — stat boosts, special abilities.  
- **Biotech rituals** — gene mods, vat-grown organs.  
- **Hacking rites** — symbolic, ritualistic deck use (no cyberspace gameplay needed).

---

## 🏚️ World Generation → Sprawl Formation  
DF worldgen → citygen:

- **Civilizations** → factions, corps, gangs.  
- **History** → turf wars, corporate coups, tech breakthroughs.  
- **Sites** → arcologies, factories, slums, data centers.  
- **Artifacts** → legendary tech items.

---

## 🧩 Final Synthesis  
You’re not rewriting DF—you’re **re-skinning its simulation**:

- Replace fantasy nouns with cyberpunk nouns.  
- Replace medieval tech trees with industrial/tech trees.  
- Replace monsters with drones/mutants.  
- Replace nobles with fixers/corp agents.  
- Replace magic with implants/biotech/hacking.  
- Replace fortress with arcology block/undercity sector.

The underlying gameplay loop—resource extraction, construction, crafting, social simulation, defense—remains unchanged.

This worldgen template gives you:

Micro systems that trigger reaction needs in the player and NPC's:
1. Need decay — the universal pressure engine
Every entity has needs that tick down:
    Hunger
    Thirst
    Fatigue
    Social
    Safety
2. Environmental drift — slow changes that create new constraints
3. Resource regeneration — the scarcity/opportunity cycle
Food, water, loot, and materials respawn slowly.
This creates:
    Competition
    Migration
    Conflict
    Exploration
4. Territory pressure — factions expand or contract
A simple “influence field” (1 byte per tile) that spreads or shrinks.
This creates:
    Border tension
    Patrols
    Raids
    Refugees
    Predator zones
5. Hazard propagation — danger spreads
Fire, corruption, poison, alarms, trace, ICE escalation.
This creates:
    Urgency
    Evacuation
    Heroic rescues
    Cascading failures
6. Item decay — equipment slowly breaks
Weapons dull, food spoils, modules corrupt.
This creates:
    Maintenance
    Scavenging
    Risk
    Improvisation
7. Memory scars — past events create future behavior

Entities remember:
    Last injury
    Last enemy
    Last safe place
    Last resource location

This creates:
    Fear loops
    Revenge arcs
    Avoidance patterns
    Emergent personalities
    
8.  Micro‑economy — simple supply/demand

Track:
    Local food supply
    Local danger
    Local population
    Local wealth

This creates:
    Migration
    Trade
    Theft
    Conflict 
9.     Opportunity pulses — periodic events
Timers generate:
    Rainfall
    Festivals
    Market days
    Predator hunts
    Patrol sweeps

10. Random faults — DF’s “glitches”
Small random events:
    Pathfinding error
    Mistake
    Panic
    Overreaction
    Dropped item

11. Micro‑quests — tiny goals that emerge from state
Examples:
    “Find water”
    “Escape predator”
    “Repair module”
    “Recover stolen item”
    “Reach safe zone”
These are generated automatically from needs + environment.
